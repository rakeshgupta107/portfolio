WPP Open: Defining Platform & UX Strategy for an AI-Powered Marketing OS

Shaped platform and UX strategy for WPP Open, driving adoption of AI agents, role-based experiences, and unified access across global marketing agencies. Focused on making an existing platform more contextual, personalized, and accessible to 100,000+ users.

The Challenge

WPP invests significantly in AI and technology innovation to support 100,000+ employees across hundreds of global marketing agencies. WPP Open was launched as the unified platform to bring together tools, data, and AI capabilities across the organization.

When I joined as Senior Product Lead, the platform was in an early stage with opportunities to establish clearer product strategy and improve adoption across agencies.

My focus: Define platform and UX strategy to make WPP Open more contextual and personalized for diverse agency roles—and drive adoption by aligning the platform with actual user workflows.

Key opportunities I focused on:

  • Product strategy definition: Establishing clear vision for how the platform should serve different roles and use cases
  • Context-aware experiences: Making AI and tools more relevant by connecting them to user role, client, and project context
  • User experience alignment: Designing experiences that matched how agency teams actually work
  • Adoption acceleration: Supporting agencies in understanding and using the platform effectively
  • Analytics consolidation: Bringing together insights from multiple sources into unified views
  • Access and onboarding: Streamlining how users get set up and gain access to relevant tools
  • Information architecture: Aligning workspace structure with how agencies, clients, and teams collaborate

The opportunity was to evolve the platform from a collection of tools into an integrated operating system that understood each user's context and needs.

My Approach

I joined as Senior Product Lead to shape the platform and UX strategy for WPP Open. My focus was on defining how the platform could become more contextual and personalized—understanding each user's role, clients, and needs to deliver more relevant experiences.

Strategic Insight: Context Drives Value

Through conversations with agencies and analysis of usage patterns, a key insight emerged: the platform needed to understand context to be truly valuable.

  • A creative director working on beverage brands needs different tools and content than a data analyst supporting automotive clients
  • An account manager at one agency has different workflows than a strategist at another
  • Users working on specific clients should see relevant tools, data, and assets—not everything

This led to the "context pass-through" strategic framework: role, client, and location context should flow seamlessly through every experience in the platform.

Strategic Framework: Context Pass-Through

The core insight was simple but powerful: the platform should know who you are, what you're working on, and what you need access to—and pass that context seamlessly to every tool and AI agent you use.

Context-aware personalization model: Role, Client, and Location context flows through to every experience

This meant the platform needed to understand three key dimensions for every user:

  • Role: Are you a strategist, creative director, data analyst, account manager?
  • Client context: Which clients and brands are you working with right now?
  • Location/agency: Which office, team, and projects are you assigned to?

Key Decisions & Trade-offs

1. Rebuild Information Architecture from User Needs

The existing hierarchical workspace structure was built around system logic, not how agencies and clients actually work. I redesigned the IA to match real relationships: agencies work with multiple clients, teams work across agencies, projects span brands within clients.

Trade-off: Required rethinking data model vs. keeping existing structure. We chose to fix it properly.

2. Context Over Customization

Rather than letting users configure their own dashboard (which nobody does), we built a smart system that inferred what they needed based on role, projects, and behavior. This reduced complexity and delivered value faster.

Trade-off: Faster time-to-value vs. flexibility. We chose speed.

3. Automate Onboarding, Not Just Workflows

Months-long manual workspace setup was killing adoption. I prioritized automated onboarding and provisioning over adding new features. Getting people productive in days instead of months mattered more than another integration.

Trade-off: Infrastructure work vs. visible features. We chose foundation.

4. Product Strategy Before Engineering

Slowed down feature development to establish proper product processes: user research, journey mapping, strategy docs, success metrics. This created short-term friction but ensured we were building the right things.

Trade-off: Velocity vs. direction. We chose direction.

What I Learned

1. Context is What Makes AI Useful in Practice

Generic AI tools have limited value for specialized work. The key learning: AI becomes genuinely useful when it has access to the same context users have—their role, current client, project brief, brand guidelines. This requires thoughtful integration, not just access to an LLM.

2. Information Architecture Shapes Everything Else

How you model relationships in a platform (agencies, clients, projects, teams) isn't just a structural decision—it determines what's possible for personalization, access control, and context-aware features. Getting the IA right early unlocks future capabilities.

3. Platform Adoption Requires Solving Real Workflow Problems

Users adopt platforms that make their jobs easier. This means understanding actual workflows, pain points, and how people collaborate—then building solutions that fit naturally into their work, not asking them to change how they work to fit the platform.

4. Role-Based Personalization Needs to Be Systematic

Creating different experiences for different roles requires systematic thinking about identity, permissions, and context management. It's infrastructure work that enables personalization at scale, not something that can be added surface-level.

5. Strategy and Execution Must Work Together

Defining strategy is valuable, but it must connect to execution. The most effective approach was working closely with agencies, professional services, and implementation teams to ensure the strategy could actually be realized and adopted.
